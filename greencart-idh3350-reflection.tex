\section{Abstract}
% ===========================================================

This document chronicles the development of \textbf{GreenCart}, a full-stack web application
built over the Spring 2025 semester as a practical environmental action project for IDH 3350 at the
University of South Florida. GreenCart addresses the \emph{attitude--behavior gap} in sustainable
consumer behavior by giving students and Tampa Bay residents the tools to discover eco-conscious
products, receive AI-generated sustainability scores for arbitrary product URLs, engage with local
environmental organizations, and collectively reflect on their purchasing habits through a
research-grade survey instrument. The project combines software engineering, behavioral science, and
community design to lower the friction between caring about sustainability and acting on that care.
This report documents the activity timeline and deliverables, describes the resulting platform and
its five core modules, reflects on how the project connects to key class concepts, and offers an
honest introspective account of what the process felt like and how it has changed my outlook as a
consumer and a builder.

\section{Introduction and Motivation}
% ===========================================================

\begin{greenbox}
  \enquote{Consumer behavior is one of the most significant drivers of environmental change in
  the modern world. Every purchasing decision, travel habit, and disposal choice made by billions
  of people adds up to a planetary impact that science can no longer ignore.}\\
  \hfill \textit{Tuan Khang Phan, ``Consumer Behavior Impacts on Environmental
  Sustainability,'' IDH 3350 research paper}
\end{greenbox}

When I enrolled in IDH 3350, I was already studying computer science and had a growing interest in
applying technology to sustainability challenges. The semester project prompt asked us to engage in
an activity that creates \emph{real positive impact} on an environment we inhabit, not a hypothetical
proposal, but something we actually build, do, and live with.

For me, the most natural response was to build a platform. I had watched family and friends struggle
to answer a simple question when shopping online: \emph{``Is this product actually good for the
environment, or is this just marketing?''} Greenwashing is rampant, eco-labels are inconsistent, and
the cognitive effort required to cross-reference certifications, read ingredient lists, and compare
carbon estimates is far beyond what the average shopper will invest at the moment of purchase.

That is the gap GreenCart is designed to close. It sits at the intersection of three circles: the
behavioral science concept of the attitude--behavior gap (which we explored extensively in class),
the emerging capability of large-language-model AI to synthesize complex product information, and
the social fabric of a student community that actually cares about these issues but lacks an easy
on-ramp to act on that care.

\section{The Resulting Product: GreenCart Platform}
% ===========================================================

GreenCart is organized into five interconnected modules. Each module addresses a distinct barrier
in the journey from environmental awareness to action.

\subsection{Module 1: AI Product Analyzer (\texttt{/analyze})}

The centerpiece of GreenCart is the AI Analyzer. A user pastes any product URL from any retailer;
the server scrapes the product page, sends the extracted content to Claude (Anthropic's large
language model), and returns a structured sustainability report containing:

\begin{itemize}
  \item An \textbf{overall sustainability score} (0--100) rendered as an animated SVG ring
  \item \textbf{Category scores} across five dimensions: materials \& ingredients, manufacturing
        processes, labor \& ethics, packaging, and durability/repairability
  \item A \textbf{carbon estimate} in kg CO\textsubscript{2}e with lifecycle breakdown and
        qualitative band (e.g., ``Low,'' ``Moderate,'' ``High'')
  \item \textbf{Strengths} and \textbf{concerns} listed as bullet points
  \item \textbf{Actionable recommendations} for the consumer
  \item \textbf{Greener alternatives} with names, links, estimated scores, and justification
\end{itemize}

The key design decision was to present AI scoring as \emph{guidance}, not certification, keeping
the user's critical judgment in the loop while dramatically reducing the time cost of sustainability
research.

\subsection{Module 2: Eco Marketplace (\texttt{/marketplace})}

The Marketplace provides a curated catalog of 190+ products tagged with sustainability scores,
resale/secondhand badges, category labels (EcoLiving, EcoTech, EcoFashion), student feedback
quotes, price, and star ratings. Users can:

\begin{itemize}
  \item Filter by category, minimum rating, maximum price, or resale-only
  \item Switch between grid and list views
  \item Click ``AI Score'' on any listing to route directly to the Analyzer
  \item Submit their own product listings (authenticated users), with optional image upload to
        Supabase storage
\end{itemize}

The explicit \textbf{resale filter} is intentional: secondhand and refurbished goods consistently
represent the highest-impact consumption choice a person can make, and surfacing them alongside
new products normalizes circular economy behavior.

\subsection{Module 3: Community Hub (\texttt{/community})}

The Community module is a three-column social interface modeled loosely on professional social
networks but focused entirely on local environmental action in Tampa Bay. It includes:

\begin{itemize}
  \item A \textbf{social feed} where authenticated users can post sustainability updates, share
        links (with auto-linked URLs), attach photos (uploaded to Supabase), tag their feeling
        (with an emoji picker), and add their location (typed or auto-detected via the browser
        Geolocation API and OpenStreetMap reverse geocoding)
  \item A \textbf{volunteer opportunity matcher} that ranks local opportunities by overlap
        with the user's selected cause interests and availability schedule
  \item A curated \textbf{Tampa Bay organization directory} with links to real local groups
        working on coastal restoration, water quality, biodiversity, and zero-waste initiatives
  \item \textbf{Trending hashtags} (e.g., \texttt{\#MangroveRestoration},
        \texttt{\#ZeroWaste}, \texttt{\#TampaBayWatch}) to create a sense of shared
        local momentum
  \item \textbf{Cause interest chips} (cleanup, marine, restoration, climate, water,
        education, biodiversity) that personalize the volunteer feed
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Module 4: Consumer Behavior Survey (\texttt{/survey})}

The survey instrument was designed to serve double duty: it provides useful data for my IDH 3350
research paper, and it prompts users to reflect on their own behavior, which is itself an
intervention. The 22 questions are organized into six sections:

\begin{enumerate}[label=\Alph*.]
  \item Environmental Awareness (4 items)
  \item Attitudes Toward Sustainable Consumption (4 items)
  \item Actual Purchasing Behavior (4 items)
  \item Social \& Peer Influence (3 items)
  \item Barriers to Sustainable Shopping (3 items)
  \item GreenCart Platform Impact (4 items)
\end{enumerate}

After submission, users receive a personalized \textbf{verdict} (e.g., ``Strongly
Pro-Sustainability,'' ``Eco-Conscious but Constrained,'' ``Low Eco-Consciousness Indicated''),
per-section averages visualized as colored bars, and a full per-question breakdown. Seeing their
own scores laid out clearly is intended to create a moment of self-reflection that nudges behavior.

\subsection{Module 5: User Guide (\texttt{/guide})}

A step-by-step onboarding page written specifically with IDH 3350 students in mind, explaining how
to use each feature, what the scores mean (and don't mean), and how GreenCart connects to the
course's sustainability concepts.

\section{Connection to Class Concepts}
% ===========================================================

\subsection{The Attitude--Behavior Gap}

Perhaps the most important concept from IDH 3350 for understanding GreenCart is the
\textbf{attitude--behavior gap}: the well-documented tendency for people to hold pro-environmental
values without translating those values into action. Research consistently shows that most consumers
\emph{care} about sustainability but are stymied by information costs, price premiums, inconvenience,
and social norms that make conventional consumption the path of least resistance
\cite{kollmuss2002mind}.

GreenCart attacks each of these friction points directly:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Information costs:} AI Analyzer produces a score in seconds instead
        of requiring hours of research.
  \item \textbf{Price barriers:} Marketplace surfaces budget-friendly resale options
        alongside new eco products.
  \item \textbf{Inconvenience:} The platform integrates discovery, scoring, and
        community in a single interface rather than requiring visits to multiple sites.
  \item \textbf{Social norms:} The Community feed, trending tags, and volunteer
        matching create visible peer activity around sustainable choices, leveraging social proof
        as a positive influence.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Information Asymmetry and Greenwashing}

A recurring theme in class was the problem of \textbf{information asymmetry} in markets: companies
know far more about the true environmental impact of their products than consumers do, and this gap
is frequently exploited through greenwashing. The AI Analyzer is, at its core, an attempt to
partially close this asymmetry. By applying a consistent scoring rubric across product categories
and surfacing specific concerns (e.g., ``Contains microplastics,'' ``No living-wage certification
visible''), it shifts the information balance toward the consumer.

The platform also acknowledges its own epistemic limits; scores are labeled as ``AI guidance,
not certification,'' which is itself an exercise in honest framing that class readings on
scientific literacy and risk communication emphasize as essential for public trust.

\subsection{Collective Action and Community Organizing}

IDH 3350 taught us that individual behavior change, while necessary, is insufficient on its own.
Sustainable futures are built through \textbf{collective action} at multiple scales: peer networks,
community organizations, advocacy, and infrastructure change. The Community module of GreenCart is
designed with this understanding. Volunteer matching is not merely a convenience feature; it is an
attempt to lower the activation energy for someone to move from passive awareness to organized
participation in Tampa Bay's environmental groups. The cause-matching algorithm prioritizes
alignment between user interests (e.g., marine restoration, climate education) and opportunity
type, making the translation from \emph{interest} to \emph{action} as frictionless as possible.

\subsection{Systems Thinking and Lifecycle Awareness}

The carbon estimate card in the Analyzer module is the most direct expression of \textbf{systems
thinking} in GreenCart. Rather than presenting a single ``eco'' badge, it breaks down CO\textsubscript{2}e
by lifecycle stage: raw materials extraction, manufacturing, transportation, use phase, and
end-of-life, and identifies the main impact drivers. This mirrors the lifecycle thinking we
discussed in class as a prerequisite for genuine environmental literacy, as opposed to the
superficial green labeling that dominates mainstream consumer culture.

\subsection{Consumer Sovereignty and Agency}

A philosophical premise running through the platform is that consumers are \textbf{not passive
recipients} of market forces but active agents whose aggregated choices shape production systems
over time. The survey's Section B (\emph{Attitudes Toward Sustainable Consumption}) explicitly
probes this belief: \enquote{I believe individual purchasing choices can make a meaningful
difference to the environment.} Seeing one's own score on this dimension, alongside the scores
for barriers and actual behavior, creates a feedback loop between self-perception and potential
agency that the platform is designed to catalyze.

% ===========================================================
\section{Impact on the Environments I Engage With}
% ===========================================================

\subsection{The Student Community (Level 2 \& 3 Scopes)}

The most immediate environment GreenCart is designed to impact is the student community at USF and
the broader Tampa Bay area. By providing a free, publicly accessible tool that reduces the effort
required to shop sustainably, the platform has the potential to shift purchasing patterns for
anyone who uses it. The $\sim$190 products in the Marketplace represent real purchasing alternatives;
the volunteer opportunities represent real organizations doing real restoration and education work;
and the social feed creates a real space for community visibility around environmental choices.

Critically, the platform is also a \textbf{shared reference point}. When I posted about GreenCart
in the IDH 3350 community and shared the research blog, I was contributing to a common vocabulary
around the attitude--behavior gap and sustainable consumption that the class had been building all
semester. A tool that makes that vocabulary actionable, turning a theoretical concept into a
button you can click, bridges the gap between the academic and the lived.

\subsection{The Information Environment}

Beyond individual behavior, GreenCart makes a small contribution to a larger and more important
project: improving the quality of sustainability information available to everyday consumers. Every
analysis run through the platform produces a structured, multi-dimensional assessment that goes
significantly beyond the binary eco/not-eco framing of most current labeling systems. If enough
people use and trust tools like this, market pressure on producers to improve their sustainability
profiles will increase, creating a systems-level feedback loop.

\subsection{My Own Environment: Skills, Habits, and Dispositions}

Building GreenCart has changed the way I personally shop. I now run product URLs through the
Analyzer before significant purchases. I have switched several of my routine purchases to
secondhand options surfaced by the Marketplace filters. More fundamentally, the act of
building the survey instrument forced me to confront my own answers to those 22 questions, and
several of my responses in Sections C and E (actual behavior and barriers) were less flattering
than my responses in Section B (attitudes). That gap is uncomfortable and instructive.

\section{Introspective Reflection}
% ===========================================================

\subsection{How It Felt to Do This Project}

Honestly, this project was one of the more demanding things I have undertaken as an undergraduate
student, not because the individual technical components were unfamiliar, but because the goal was
to build something \emph{real}: something that would actually be used, that would reflect my own
values in its design decisions, and that would be evaluated not only on technical execution but on
genuine impact.

There were weeks when the gap between what I had built and what I imagined was discouraging. The
AI scraping pipeline frequently ran into retailer bot-detection; the Supabase schema needed to be
redesigned twice; the Community module took far longer than expected because designing a social
experience that feels natural is genuinely hard. But there was also something deeply motivating
about working toward a problem I actually care about. Knowing that the survey questions would
produce data relevant to a paper I had written for this class gave the code a weight it would
not have had if I were building a generic todo app.

One moment stands out in particular. About ten weeks in, I ran the Analyzer on a product I had
purchased a month earlier and was surprised to find a sustainability score of 28 out of 100, with
concerns including non-recyclable composite materials and supply chain opacity. I had bought it
without thinking. That moment of confronting my own behavior through a tool I had built was
strange and clarifying in equal measure. It is one thing to understand the attitude--behavior gap
as a concept; it is another to see your own score.

\subsection{How This Project Has Changed Me}

Before this semester, I thought of sustainability as a domain issue, something for environmental
science students, policy researchers, or activists. Building GreenCart has convinced me that it is
equally, maybe more urgently, a \textbf{design and information problem}. The environmental outcomes
we care about are downstream of millions of small decisions made by people who are not experts,
who are busy, and who are navigating markets designed to obscure the information they need to
decide well. Making that information legible, accessible, and actionable is work that technologists
and designers are uniquely positioned to do, and it is work that matters.

I have also become more comfortable with what I would call \textbf{productive humility}: the
platform does not solve greenwashing, it does not replace lifecycle assessments, and its AI scores
are imperfect. But imperfect, accessible, and free is often more impactful than perfect,
inaccessible, and paywalled. Naming that clearly in the platform's own copy (``AI guidance, not
certification'') was an important lesson in honest communication under uncertainty; something the
class reading on scientific literacy and public trust made very clear.

\subsection{Will I Continue?}

Yes, without question. The most significant outcome of this project is not the platform itself
(though I intend to keep it running and improving), but the clarity it has given me about what I
want to do with my skills. I am planning to:

\begin{itemize}
  \item Continue developing GreenCart post-semester, with a focus on making the survey
        data collection more robust (server-side persistence to Supabase, aggregate
        visualizations, shareable results).
  \item Expand the community module to include real-time events posted by Tampa Bay
        environmental organizations.
  \item Build additional AI analysis dimensions, including a supply chain transparency
        score and a circularity index.
  \item Share the platform actively with student organizations at USF and HCC as a
        practical tool for sustainability education.
  \item Explore whether the platform's survey data, if enough responses are collected,
        could become the basis of a more formal undergraduate research project.
\end{itemize}

More broadly, I intend to keep asking the question this project taught me to ask: \emph{Where is
the gap between what people care about and what they do, and how can better design and information
close it?} That question is not limited to sustainability. But sustainability is where I found it
first, and it is where the stakes are highest.

% ===========================================================
\section{Conclusion}
% ===========================================================

GreenCart began as a semester project and became, over fourteen weeks, a real tool that real people
can use to make more informed choices about the products they buy and the communities they join. It
is an expression of a conviction this class helped me develop: that engaged citizens do not only
vote and volunteer and reduce and reuse; they also build. They build platforms and instruments and
communities and knowledge systems that make sustainable living easier for the people around them.

The attitude--behavior gap is real. Information asymmetry is real. The inconvenience of sustainable
consumption is real. But none of these are fixed features of the world. They are design problems,
and design problems have solutions. GreenCart is one small attempt at a few of those solutions, and
building it has made me a more intentional consumer, a more honest communicator, and a more
committed practitioner of the sustainable futures we discussed in class.

\section{Survey Instrument (Full Question List)}
% ===========================================================

\textbf{Scale:} 1 = Strongly Disagree \quad 2 = Moderately Disagree \quad 3 = Neutral \quad
4 = Moderately Agree \quad 5 = Strongly Agree

\bigskip
\textbf{Section A: Environmental Awareness}
\begin{enumerate}[label=A\arabic*.]
  \item I am aware of the environmental impact of the products I purchase daily.
  \item I regularly look for eco-labels or sustainability certifications when shopping.
  \item I understand what the terms ``carbon footprint'' and ``circular economy'' mean.
  \item I feel informed enough to make environmentally responsible purchasing decisions.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Section B: Attitudes Toward Sustainable Consumption}
\begin{enumerate}[label=B\arabic*.]
  \item Buying sustainably made products is important to me personally.
  \item I believe individual purchasing choices can make a meaningful difference to the environment.
  \item I would pay a premium for a product that is certified as environmentally friendly.
  \item I prefer buying secondhand or refurbished products over buying new when possible.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Section C: Actual Purchasing Behavior}
\begin{enumerate}[label=C\arabic*.]
  \item In the past month, I consciously avoided buying a product due to environmental concerns.
  \item I actively reduce the amount of single-use plastics I purchase.
  \item I research a brand's environmental practices before making a significant purchase.
  \item I choose local or low-packaging options at grocery stores or markets.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Section D: Social \& Peer Influence}
\begin{enumerate}[label=D\arabic*.]
  \item My friends and peers influence my decision to buy eco-friendly products.
  \item I have shared information about sustainable products or practices on social media.
  \item I feel social pressure to appear environmentally conscious in my choices.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Section E: Barriers to Sustainable Shopping}
\begin{enumerate}[label=E\arabic*.]
  \item Price is the biggest barrier preventing me from buying more eco-friendly products.
  \item It is difficult to find trustworthy information about a product's sustainability.
  \item I find it inconvenient to shop sustainably compared to conventional shopping.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Section F: GreenCart Platform Impact}
\begin{enumerate}[label=F\arabic*.]
  \item A platform like GreenCart that scores product sustainability would change how I shop.
  \item Seeing a sustainability score on a product page would make me more likely to choose an
        eco-friendly option.
  \item Access to greener alternatives suggested by AI would help me make better purchasing
        decisions.
  \item Community features (volunteer matching, social feed) motivate me to take environmental
        action.
\end{enumerate}
